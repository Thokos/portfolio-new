Why this project?

Whenever my (hockey) team has a training or a match to play we spam our Whatsapp group with a long list of who can attend the training or match and who can’t. However, during the Whatsapp messaging, we are still faced with questions such as:

  • Are we playing in our home kit or away kit?

  • At what time do we play?

  • At what time do we gather and where do we play?

This is annoying as these questions result in new questions and remarks and not the desired solution. It’s also time-consuming so I asked myself the question of a separate app that could solve this issue. After some initial thinking, I came up with the idea to make an application that may solve the communication stress within my team.

What’s my role?

How I started

I started by writing down the screens I felt were needed in the app solution Afterward, I wrote down the functionalities and my tasks for this application.

After the overview was completed, and I envisioned how the screens needed to be designed, I started to separate the screens into sprints. This will make the work easier to plan and execute. Subsequently, this made it possible to set several deadlines.

Every sprint contains the screens I planned to design. First, I made sketches of the screens, after which I made some low-fidelity screens which later on could be designed into high-fidelity screens.

1. First sprint

I started the first sprint with the design of the login screen, creating an account and a team both as the entry point of the app. First, I summarized the content needed for every screen (for example which input fields, headers, buttons are needed). After I completed that step, I started to make sketches of these screens for the next step.

Once I completed these sketches, I made a user flow to display how the user is going to navigate through the first few screens.

Then it was time to turn the sketches into low fidelity screens. I used Figma for this task (all the low and high-fidelity screens were done in Figma). 

Before I started with the high-fidelity screens, I had to decide which font, which colors, and other input styles I was going to use. That's why I started to make a style guide to set the guides for the development of “The Hockeyplanner App.” which is the current name of the app.

3. Third Sprint

The third sprint is where everything started to come together. First, I started evaluating what needed to be done for every screen. I will start with the leaderboard screen as that screen needs some further explanation. 

The leaderboard screen was the most challenging because I wanted to use gamification in this app to motivate the users to change their availability for a training or a match. This is essential to the success of the app for the users and it gives other teammates insight in who is attending a training/match or not. In games which are popular nowadays (such as Fortnite and Call of Duty) seasons and battle passes* are very popular.

*Battle passes contain some rewards you get after playing a certain game and they give rewards to players/users for completing certain tasks and to motivate them as team members.

Above you can see how you can collect these points. I did some research on what kind of activity (training/match) the availability was the lowest. It turns out that on average the players/users who attend a hockey match is about 35% higher than attending a hockey training. As this has some impact on the team as a whole, I decided to give more points for attending a training versus a match. You can also score points for assists given and goals scored. The captain of the team must enter this into the app right after the match or training. 

Since most competitive sports already work with seasons it's a good thing to add this feature in the Hockey Planner App as well. The team captain can set a reward and a timer for the start of the season and the end of the season. The reward can’t be changed during the season.

On top of that, I also added the possibility to earn badges which all the players can earn if they complete certain tasks (for example; score 100 points). Below you see the sketches, wireframes and high-fidelity screens I made for this solution.

Settings were also included in sprint 3. This screen is straightforward with the most important functions for every player.

5. Conclusions and learning points


What I learned

I learned quite a lot of things during this process and project. One of the things was making a switch from Sketch to Figma. Figma is a upcoming design tool and I like the UI more than the UI of Sketch. This is the first full project I designed in Figma. 

I learned how to brainstorm and transform text written down on paper to a working prototype. 

I also read a lot about gamification lately which I could us during the whole project as it helped me to try newly gained knowledge for this app.

At this moment it is still too soon to decide if this app can and will be used for my hockey team in the near future. I can see the potential of the app but it needs a bit more research and testing with my teammates.

What would I do different next time

I will most certainly work with components on my next project. At first, I thought I could do without it but when I was in the middle of the project I decided to change the inputs and buttons. I could have saved a lot of time if I just made some components at the start.

Also when I'm making a prototype I won't do it till the very end. I found a lot of edge cases when I was prototyping.
